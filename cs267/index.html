High-performance parallel RTL simulation for Chisel

I'm a first-year PhD student working in the ASPIRE Lab. I work with the computer architecture research group, and I've had some involvement with the Chisel project. This project aims to increase the productivity of digital circuit designers by raising the abstraction level of RTL design using a metaprogramming-oriented HDL embedded in Scala. One interesting facet of the Chisel project is that its software simulation infrastructure involves a C++ backend to generate a custom cycle-accurate simulator of the user's RTL design. Although this generated simulator has been shown to be competitive with commercial implementations, limited work has been done on parallelizing its execution.

I think an interesting application of parallel computing is the optimization of event-driven RTL simulators. The current techniques for finding parallelization at the application level include design-level parallelism, where the HDL input is partitioned into event sequences capable in executing in parallel, and low-level data-parallel operations. One interesting aspect of this mode of simulation is that it is necessarily exact: due to the use of cycle-level time granularity and logic-level voltage granularity, cycle-accurate simulations can be performed with perfect fidelity at the RTL level, excluding the influence of soft and hard logic errors.

Although many projects in parallelizing HDL simulation have been undertaken in the past, state-of-the-art commercial implementations such as Synopsis VCS claim only modest 2x speedups with parallelization. The primary platform for commercial parallel simulators is an SMP server, with clusters of such servers used for job-level parallelism or MapReduce-like division into independent, parallel simulations. Currently, several active research projects are exploring the use of GPUs in accelerating RTL simulation, but the field does not seem to have been exhaustively mined, despited the massive resources of the EDA industry. A more thorough study of this problem might yield better results.

The main challenge to scaling the application to large numbers of processors is the tight coupling of the different nodes of an RTL design graph. This application would certainly require efficient and effective methods for clustering the problem to obtain the maximum possible design-level parallelism, especially if strong scaling is desired for moderate-size designs.
